# Week 1: Engines, Anatomy, and Choosing Yours

> **How to use this module:** Everything you need to complete this week's work is in this module. The live lectures walk the same ground off script, so treat them as a bonus, not a requirement. Read the concept material, follow the practice guide in your engine, and check the assignment spec before you build.

**Role in the course:** Orientation. Before you write a line of code, you should know what a game engine actually is, what every engine has in common, and which one you'll live in all semester.

## Learning objectives
- Describe the major subsystems every engine provides (rendering, scene management, physics, input, audio, asset pipeline, scripting runtime).
- Compare Unity, Godot, Unreal, and Three.js: languages, licensing, platform targets, strengths.
- Install and verify a working development environment in your chosen engine.
- Use an LLM (through a chatbot) as a tutor for learning an unfamiliar engine.

## Concept material (to be written)
1. What is a game engine? Why they exist, and what you'd have to build without one. (Spoiler: this entire course, plus a renderer.)
2. **The Common Anatomy:** a subsystem-by-subsystem tour of what every engine ships.
3. **Engine Profiles:** Unity, Godot, Unreal, Three.js, with honorable mentions for Phaser and MonoGame.
4. How to choose: your goals, your language comfort, your hardware, your lane for the final project.
5. The engine policy in practice: what "your engine is your responsibility" means day to day.
6. Prompting an AI to teach you an engine: what works, and what to distrust.
