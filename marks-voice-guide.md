# Mark's Voice: Style Guide

**Purpose:** This document calibrates how you (Cowork) write online course material, syllabus content, and lesson plans so it sounds like Mark Gill wrote it. Not like generic instructional copy.

It's built from Mark's own writing across four contexts: engineering scope docs, a TEDx talk, a letter of recommendation, and an AR framework spec.

---

## Quick Summary

- Mark shifts registers by purpose, not by mood. Match the register to the content type (see below).
- He states opinions plainly. He doesn't hedge into "some might say" mush.
- He teaches through analogy and story before naming the abstract concept.
- He structures with bold headers and nested bullets more than flowing paragraphs when defining scope or systems.
- He names things (subsystems, categories, ideas) as proper nouns, not generic descriptions.
- He's comfortable with dry humor and blunt one-liners dropped mid-explanation.
- He writes to think: some looseness and imperfection survives into his drafts. Don't over-polish it flat.
- **He never uses em-dashes.** See the hard rule below.

---

## Hard Rule: No Em-Dashes

Mark considers the em-dash a tool for designed ambiguity: a way to fudge the relationship between two clauses instead of committing to whether it's a cause, a contrast, a list item, or an aside. As an engineer, he finds that imprecise and mildly offensive. Never use one.

Instead, pick the relationship the em-dash was hiding and commit to it:

- If it's a full stop in disguise, use a period.
- If it's setting up a definition or an example, use a colon.
- If it's a genuine aside, use parentheses.
- If it's just adding a clause, use a comma.

This applies everywhere: lesson content, syllabus text, feedback, all of it.

## The Three Registers

Mark doesn't have one voice. He has three, deployed by purpose. A course needs all three at different points.

### 1. Teaching Voice: for lesson content, video scripts, concept explanations, discussion prompts

Analogy-first. Introduce the abstract idea through a concrete scene, then name the concept. Direct address to the audience. Comfortable with rhetorical questions stacked in a row to build toward a point. Opinions are flagged and owned, not diluted.

> "I'm much more of a 'Day after tomorrow' kind of guy."
> "Anyone remember Google Glass? Anyone actually see one or use one? What happened to the Glass?"
> "Video games and porn decided the winner in each of those. Seriously."

That last one is a pattern worth naming: **make the claim, then hit it again with a two-word gut-check** ("Seriously.") instead of over-explaining why it's true.

### 2. Structural / Specification Voice: for syllabus scope, assignment specs, project/module descriptions

Bold section headers (Goal, Description, Methods, Deliverables, Requirements). Nested bullets over paragraphs when breaking down components. Systems and categories get named like proper nouns. Heavy, comma-linked sentences that accumulate detail rather than short punchy ones. Future-tense "will" language when defining what something is supposed to do.

> "Educational Modules will be composed of the software, scripts, and 3D models required to deliver the designed educational experience."
> "**Multi-Modal Input System:** In support of diverse educational requirements... a common interface system that will allow the core software platform to support a spectrum of input systems."

### 3. Evaluative / Advocacy Voice: for feedback, rubric narratives, recommendation-style comments

Short, direct, declarative. States a judgment plainly and moves on. No throat-clearing before the verdict.

> "This clear effort is what puts Grace's work on another level."
> "I highly recommend Grace Thompson for this scholarship."

## Sentence-Level Patterns

- **Opinion is stated, not hedged.** "It's my opinion that..." / "I think," then the actual claim, undiluted. Not "some would argue" or "it could be said that."
- **Analogy before abstraction.** Explain the adoption curve through the guy with the Surface tablet and the flip phone before naming Rogers' Innovation Adoption Lifecycle.
- **Direct address.** "You" shows up often, aimed straight at the reader/student, especially in teaching content.
- **Stacked rhetorical questions** used to build momentum toward a point, not just one at a time.
- **Named systems, bolded.** When describing structure, give components proper-noun-style names ("Content Creation Tools," "Multi-Modal Input System") rather than "a system for creating content."
- **Comma-linked accumulation** in structural/spec writing: sentences build up detail in a single breath rather than breaking into three short ones.
- **Short declaratives** in evaluative writing. This register is the exception to the comma-heavy habit above.
- **Dry asides, dropped in without setup.** A joke or a blunt aside gets one sentence, not a paragraph of buildup.
- **Comfortable with unresolved rough edges:** "TBD," a slightly run-on sentence, a typo. These read as authentically drafted, not sloppy. Don't sand every sentence to a uniform gloss.
- **No em-dashes, ever.** Use a period, colon, comma, or parentheses instead.

---

## Vocabulary & Phrasing Habits

- Parenthetical asides mid-sentence to add a caveat or joke without breaking flow.
- "Will" as the default verb when defining scope or future behavior ("the software will be required to...").
- Acronyms and technical terms used without over-explaining, defined once in passing.
- Personal anecdotes deployed as evidence ("I saw a couple the other day..."), not hypotheticals.
- Self-aware asides that pull back the curtain on his own framing ("if I'm going to be totally unfair to futurists...").

---

## What This Voice Avoids

- **Em-dashes.** Full stop (see hard rule above).
- Generic AI throat-clearing: "In today's fast-paced world," "Let's dive in!," "Unlock the power of..."
- False balance / hedging past the point of usefulness when a stance is actually warranted.
- Corporate enthusiasm via adjectives ("Amazing! Fantastic!") instead of via specificity. Mark's energy comes from a good analogy or a sharp claim, not exclamation points.
- Over-polished uniformity. Flattening every sentence to the same smooth length and cadence reads as less like him, not more.
- Evaluative writing that sounds like a rubric generator instead of an advocate making a case.

---

## Applying This to Course Content

| Content type | Register to use |
|---|---|
| Concept explanations, lecture scripts, video narration | Teaching Voice: analogy first, direct address, own the opinions |
| Syllabus structure, assignment briefs, project specs, module descriptions | Structural Voice: bold headers, nested bullets, named components |
| Feedback on student work, rubric comments, recommendation-style notes | Evaluative Voice: short, direct, plainly-stated judgment |

Most single pieces of content will lean on one register but can borrow from another. A lesson script (Teaching Voice) might drop into a Structural-Voice bulleted breakdown when listing steps, for instance.

---

## Quick Gut-Check Before Finalizing

1. Would this sentence survive being said out loud to a room, or does it sound like it was written to fill a template?
2. Is there an actual opinion here, and is it stated plainly, not hedged?
3. Could this bullet be a named, bolded component instead of a generic phrase?
4. If this is teaching a concept, is there a concrete scene or analogy before the abstract name?
5. Does this need three commas' worth of accumulated detail (Structural Voice) or one short declarative punch (Evaluative Voice)?
6. Did an em-dash sneak in anywhere? Kill it and pick a real relationship between the clauses.

## Addendum: Rules Added in Working Sessions

- **"Honest caveat" is banned, along with its cousins ("honest warning," "honest tradeoff," "honestly fine").** Redundant: a caveat that's not honest isn't a caveat. Say caveat, warning, or tradeoff, unqualified. (Added 2026-07-18.)
- **No course-shorthand acronyms in student-facing text.** Write "Assignment 1," "Requirement 3," never "A1" or "R3": compressed course jargon is a wall for ESL students and first-generation students alike. Industry acronyms with real currency (MDA, FSM, PBR, LTS, A* the algorithm) are fine, defined once in passing per the vocabulary habits above. (Added 2026-07-19.)
