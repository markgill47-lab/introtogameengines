# SE 266 Course Resources: The Free Library

**How to use this list:** There is no textbook for this course. There are, instead, some excellent free ones, each assigned where it's strongest, plus a shelf of deeper material for when a topic hooks you. Everything here is legally free to read or watch: full texts posted by their authors, open licenses, or publicly posted talks. Each weekly module ends with a "Going deeper" section pointing into this list; this page is the whole library in one place.

---

## The Core Stack (assigned reading lives here)

**Game Programming Patterns**, Robert Nystrom. The closest thing this course has to a required text, and the book the B tier is standing on: his chapters are literally named Command, Observer, and State, plus Game Loop, Update Method, and Component. Free to read in full at [gameprogrammingpatterns.com](https://gameprogrammingpatterns.com/). Read it in the browser; if you love it, the paid copies fund an author who gave the field a free classic. *(Prose is CC BY-NC-ND: read and share, don't republish modified copies. Code samples are MIT.)*

**Procedural Content Generation in Games**, Shaker, Togelius, and Nelson. The academic standard on procgen, with all twelve author-version chapters posted free at [pcgbook.com](https://www.pcgbook.com/), by explicit arrangement with the publisher. Week 14's companion, and the tutorial lane's deep end.

**Artificial Intelligence and Games**, Yannakakis and Togelius. A complete Springer textbook with the full PDF free at [gameaibook.org](https://gameaibook.org/). Week 15 uses its behavior-authoring sections; the rest goes far past this course, which is what shelves are for.

**MDA: A Formal Approach to Game Design and Game Research**, Hunicke, LeBlanc, and Zubek. The original five-page paper behind Week 4's framework, free: [PDF](https://users.cs.northwestern.edu/~hunicke/MDA.pdf). Citing the primary source in your A12 is a good look.

**2D Game Development: From Zero To Hero.** A 500+ page community compendium, engine-agnostic, CC BY-NC-SA, free PDF/EPub via [its GitHub](https://github.com/2DGD-F0TH/2DGD_F0TH). Depth varies chapter to chapter; a useful second angle on loops, collision, and algorithms, especially for code-first engine students.

## The Reference Shelf

**Red Blob Games** (Amit Patel), [redblobgames.com](https://www.redblobgames.com/): the best interactive explanations anywhere of A* and pathfinding, grids and hexes, and [making maps with noise](https://www.redblobgames.com/maps/terrain-from-noise/). When Week 14 or 15 makes you curious about the math, start here.

**3D Math Primer for Graphics and Game Development**, Dunn and Parberry: the full text, free at [gamemath.com](https://gamemath.com/). Vectors, matrices, transforms, and rotations at whatever depth Week 2 left you wanting. The quaternion chapter is where "never edit the four numbers" gets its mathematical why.

**Gaffer On Games** (Glenn Fiedler), [gafferongames.com](https://gafferongames.com/): the classic articles on game timing and networking. [Fix Your Timestep!](https://gafferongames.com/post/fix_your_timestep/) is Week 3's two-clocks section, argued by the person who made the argument famous; the networked physics series is where multiplayer curiosity should start. His curated [GameNetworkingResources](https://github.com/gafferongames/GameNetworkingResources) list is the netcode rabbit hole, mapped.

**Godot's official documentation**, [docs.godotengine.org](https://docs.godotengine.org/): openly licensed (CC BY) and conceptually excellent even if you never touch Godot: reading its explanations of scene trees, signals, and physics is a free second angle on half this course's dictionary tables.

## Deeper Water (tutorial lane, final projects, and hooked students)

**Shaders.** [The Book of Shaders](https://thebookofshaders.com/) (Vigo and Lowe) is the gentle, gorgeous, in-browser introduction to fragment shaders: read it with the editor open and your code running live. From there, [Catlike Coding](https://catlikecoding.com/unity/tutorials/) is the Unity-specific path (rendering, shaders, compute, and procedural work, in famously careful prose), and [LearnOpenGL's shader chapters](https://learnopengl.com/Getting-started/Shaders) are the from-first-principles version.

**PBR materials.** [LearnOpenGL's PBR section](https://learnopengl.com/PBR/Theory) is the standard free walkthrough of physically based rendering theory and implementation: what those Metallic and Smoothness sliders in your engine actually mean, derived. Heavier but authoritative: Google's [Filament PBR guide](https://google.github.io/filament/Filament.html), the working notes of a production renderer, free.

**Inverse kinematics and procedural animation.** [Alan Zucconi's IK series](https://www.alanzucconi.com/tag/inverse-kinematics/) ([2D](https://www.alanzucconi.com/2018/05/02/ik-2d-1/), [3D](https://www.alanzucconi.com/2020/09/14/inverse-kinematics-in-3d/), and [procedural animation](https://www.alanzucconi.com/2017/04/17/procedural-animations/)) is the free, readable path from Week 8's animation states to legs that place their own feet. His whole site is a tutorial-lane menu by itself.

**Game feel, on video.** The two talks Week 7 is quietly built on, free on YouTube: *Juice it or lose it* (Jonasson and Purho) and Vlambeer's *The Art of Screenshake* (Nijman). Search those titles; watch both; they're twenty minutes each and they'll change how you see every game you play.

**Procedural everything, on video.** Sebastian Lague's YouTube series (procedural terrain, pathfinding, and a dozen "Coding Adventure" episodes) are the best visual companions to Weeks 14 and 15 in existence, free.

*A closing note on the economics: every item here is free because someone decided teaching the field mattered more than paywalling it. When one of these resources carries you somewhere, the professional courtesies are: cite them in your build notes, buy the paid copy when you can someday, and pay the habit forward.*
