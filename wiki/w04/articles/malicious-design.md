# Malicious Design

Everything up to this page assumed good faith. We build [[mda|mechanics]] to cause an experience the player actually wants: mastery, flow, discovery, tension they signed up for. That is the whole job, and it is an honest one. This page is about the people who took the exact same toolkit and aimed it at your wallet instead of your enjoyment. I am not neutral on this, and by the end of this page I do not want you to be either.

Let me say the ugly part first, because it is the entire point: **the move is [[mda|MDA]], weaponized.** Same three layers. Same trace, feeling to behavior to rule. One difference, and it is the whole difference: the aesthetic on the far end is not fun. It is compulsion. It is the itch, the dread of missing the daily reward, the sunk cost you cannot make yourself walk away from. Every mechanic in the previous articles can be tuned to produce that instead of joy, and an entire industry has done exactly that, deliberately, with data scientists and A/B tests and retention dashboards. They are very good at it. That is what makes them dangerous, not harmless.

## The playbook, named

You have seen every one of these. Naming them is how you stop falling for them.

- **The Free Hook.** Free to install, free to play, instant everything. The first one is always free; that phrasing is not an accident, and neither is this business model.
- **The Onboarding Dopamine.** The opening hours are a firework show. Progression is swift, rewards land every few seconds, the whole screen lights up and congratulates you. This is real game design doing real work, and it is being spent to build a habit, not to respect your time.
- **The Wall.** Once you are invested, and only then, the game reveals the thing it hid during onboarding: a daily energy cap, a progression gate, a difficulty spike that appears out of nowhere. The generosity was a loan. Now comes the collection.
- **The Time Tax.** The wall has a door, and the door is a clock. Wait twenty-four hours, or do not. Because right there, conveniently, is a ten-dollar treasure chest of tokens that skips the wait. This is the oldest con in the catalog with a fresh coat of paint: manufacture the pain, then sell the cure for the pain you manufactured.
- **Pay-to-Win.** The least offensive tier, and it still is not good. Progression is swift and easy as long as the credit card approves. The game is honest about being a store; it just forgot to mention that the fun was the thing being rationed.
- **The Slot Machine.** Loot boxes, gacha pulls, spin-to-win: [[verbs-resources-feedback|variable rewards]] tuned to the same reinforcement schedule that makes actual slot machines illegal to sell to children. You are not buying an item. You are buying a pull, and the maybe is the product.

## Why this is a feedback loop, and why that matters

[[verbs-resources-feedback|Tetris runs a loop of doom]], and it is honest: mistakes cost you space, space runs out, and the only way out of the spiral is skill you earned. Every escape is yours. These games run a loop of doom too, and it is dishonest: the pressure mounts, the wall closes in, and the escape is not skill. The escape is your wallet. The "skill" they are selling you is a payment method.

That is the tell, and it is the whole tell. When you feel a game's pressure and ask "how do I get out of this," an honest game answers *get better*. A malicious one answers *get out your card*.

## The floor, and who is standing on it

There is a bottom to this, and it is worse than the mobile-strategy stuff. Star Trek Fleet Command will happily separate a Trek fan from a few hundred dollars, and shame on Paramount for licensing their name to it, but at least it is selling spaceships to people who came for spaceships. The floor is lower than that. Lately my phone keeps showing me ads for real-money "lucky" slots apps, one called Tang Luck, and the pitch is not subtle: *having a hard time paying your bills? Play this slots game that pays out big.* Read that twice. The design is not aimed at people with money to burn. It is aimed, on purpose, at people who cannot afford to lose and are desperate enough to hope. That is not a game with a dark pattern in it. That is a slot machine wearing a game as a disguise, hunting the people least able to defend themselves. It makes me furious, and it should make you furious, and now you have the vocabulary to say exactly why instead of just feeling that something is wrong.

## The line, for you specifically

Here is why this belongs in a design-vocabulary week and not a rant blog. You are about to learn to build these mechanics. Feedback loops, variable rewards, resource pressure, progression gates: they are on your syllabus, because they are the real tools of the trade. And they work. That is precisely the problem. The knife that carves your dinner is the knife that could do the other thing, and no one is going to take it away from you.

So the line is not the mechanic. The line is consent. Ask it every time: **am I producing an experience the player would thank me for, or manufacturing a behavior they would switch off if the game let them?** Build for the first player. The one who finishes your game, feels the thing you were chasing, and walks away richer for the time. Not the one you are trying to trap.

This is the other reason [[scope|small, finished, and polished]] is the value this whole course is built on. A game designed around an aesthetic the player consents to does not need a wall, a clock, or a chest. It needs to be good, and then to end. Make that.
