# The Promise Grid

This whole week argues one idea from a dozen angles: a game is not a genre, it is a **bundle of promises**. "RPG" and "roguelike" are shipping labels. The real thing a player signs up for is a specific set of promises about what they will do, who they will do it with, how the game is shaped around them, and how it is all supposed to feel.

So here is the bundle, taken apart into its four wires. Build a game out of four promises and watch what happens.

## The four rows

Each row is one question every game answers, and the four rows are chosen to be *independent*: knowing a game's answer to one tells you almost nothing about its answer to the next. That independence is the point. Genres are not on this grid. Genres are what you get when you pick a promise from each row and the combination turns out to be popular enough to earn a name.

- **What you do** is the core verb, the thing your hands repeat: Fight, Command, Explore, Solve. This is the [[verbs-resources-feedback|verb]] at the heart of the [[core-loop|core loop]].
- **Who with** is the social shape: Solo, Co-op, Versus, Massive. This one does the most work, because it decides whether other human beings are in the room, and that changes everything about how a promise gets kept.
- **How it is shaped** is the container: a Campaign that ends, a Persistent world that does not, a Run-based loop that resets, a Match that is over in minutes.
- **How it feels** is the target [[mda|aesthetic]], the feeling you are actually selling: Mastery, Cozy, Tension, Power.

Pick Fight, Solo, Persistent, Mastery and you have described Soulsborne without ever naming it. Pick Command, Versus, Match, Mastery and you have described the real-time strategy genre. The names fall out of the promises, not the other way around.

## Reading the shrink

Start with one promise and about a third of the library still qualifies. Add a second and it collapses. By the third or fourth promise you are usually down to a handful of titles, sometimes exactly one, and that collapse is the lesson: **a game is specific.** Four honest promises is enough to pin almost any game in the industry to a single point. When students say their design "is like a mix of everything," this is the grid that shows them everything is four small decisions, and they have not made them yet.

## The gap hunt

Now do the harder thing. Try to build a bundle that lands on **zero games.** They are not rare. Four promises carve the space into more corners than a hundred games can fill, and the empty corners come in two very different flavors, which is the entire point of hunting them.

- **A contradiction** is a gap that *should* be empty. Cozy and Mastery pull against each other: the promise of "relax, no pressure" fights the promise of "get good or lose." Fight and Cozy rarely coexist because combat wants stakes. When you hit one of these, you have not found a missing game, you have found two promises that cannot be kept at the same time. Knowing which promises fight is half of design.
- **A market gap** is a gap that is empty *by accident.* Nothing about Solve, Versus, and Cozy is contradictory, yet the corner is nearly bare, populated by a lone party game or two. A cozy competitive game is not impossible. It is under-built. Some of these corners are empty because the game would be bad, and some are empty because nobody has made it yet, and telling those two apart is the most valuable instinct a designer can have. That is where an original pitch comes from.

## Watch the player light up

The bottom strip maps your bundle onto [[bartle|Bartle's four players]]. Pick Versus and the **Killer** lights, because you just promised a human opponent who minds losing. Pick Co-op or Massive and the **Socializer** lights. Mastery or Power lights the **Achiever**; Explore or a Persistent world lights the **Explorer.** Light up three at once and you have built something that tries to please everyone, which is what a big online world reaches for and what a one-semester project cannot afford. The grid is quietly asking the [[bartle|design check]] the whole time: which player is this for, and did you choose that on purpose?

## Check the data yourself

Every game in the grid was tagged by hand against the rubric above, not pulled from a single external list. If a placement looks wrong to you, or you want to keep building the corpus, these are good places to check a game's genre, mode, and reception against a second opinion:

- **[IGDB](https://www.igdb.com)** ("Internet Game Database"): genres, game modes, and platforms per title, closest match to the *What you do* and *Who with* rows.
- **[MobyGames](https://www.mobygames.com)**: the deepest catalog for older and retro titles, useful for anything from the pre-2000 era.
- **[Steam Popular Tags](https://store.steampowered.com/tag/browse)**: crowd-applied tags on the actual store pages, a decent gut check on what a game sells itself as, which is exactly the *How it feels* row.
- **[RAWG](https://rawg.io)**: a searchable, filterable database if you want to browse by genre rather than look up one title at a time.
- **[Bartle's taxonomy on Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bartle_taxonomy_of_player_types)**: the primary reference for the player-type mapping under the grid.
- **[The MDA paper](https://users.cs.northwestern.edu/~hunicke/MDA.pdf)** (Hunicke, LeBlanc, Zubek): the same source already cited in [[mda|MDA]], for the *How it feels* row specifically.

None of these are the single source of truth. They disagree with each other constantly, which is itself a useful lesson: genre is a convention, not a fact, and the grid's tags are one designer's read, not a verdict.
