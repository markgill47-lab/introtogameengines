# Bartle's Taxonomy: Four Kinds of Player

"Fun" is not one thing, because "player" is not one person. Hand the same open-world game to four people and ask what they did, and you get four different games back. One ground out every achievement and hit 100%. One wandered straight to the edge of the map to see what was there. One spent the whole night in voice chat and barely touched the quests. One went hunting other players for sport. Same game, four experiences, because four kinds of player.

Richard Bartle, studying the text-based multiplayer worlds (MUDs) that came before modern MMOs, sorted players into four types. It is the oldest and most-quoted player-motivation model in the field. Like every such model it is a lens, not a law, but it is a genuinely useful lens, and it pairs cleanly with everything else this week.

## The four types

- **Achievers** (Bartle's Diamonds) *act on the world.* They want points, levels, completion, mastery, the platinum trophy. The accomplishment is the point, and it has to be measurable and provable. Give them clear goals and a scoreboard that means something.
- **Explorers** (Spades) *interact with the world.* They want to map it, understand its systems, break it in interesting ways, and find the secret nobody else found. The knowledge is the trophy. Give them depth and reward the poking.
- **Socializers** (Hearts) *interact with other players.* They came for the people; the game is the excuse to be in a room together. Give them cooperation, chat, and things worth doing side by side.
- **Killers** (Clubs) *act on other players.* They want to impose themselves: to compete against, and win against, a human being who minds losing. Give them a fair arena and a real opponent. Note the sharp edge here: the same drive produces both the great competitive player and the griefer, and which one you get is a design decision, not a player defect.

## The useful part: the two axes

The four types are not a random list. They sit on two axes: **acting versus interacting**, and **the world versus other players.**

- Achievers *act on the world.*
- Explorers *interact with the world.*
- Killers *act on players.*
- Socializers *interact with players.*

Draw that two-by-two once, on the back of anything, and you will never lose it.

## The limits

- It was built for **multiplayer** worlds. The two player-facing types, Socializers and Killers, map awkwardly onto single-player games, where "other players" is a leaderboard at most. Do not force all four onto a game that has no other people in it.
- **Nobody is one type.** Everyone is a blend, and the blend shifts with mood and with the specific game. You are an Explorer in a game you just met and an Achiever in the one you love.
- Bartle himself has said it gets over-applied. It is a conversation starter, not a personality test. Use it to think, not to file people.

## Why it belongs in this week

- **It is a design check.** Ask: which types am I serving, which am I ignoring, and is that on purpose? A game that tries to serve all four usually serves none of them well. Most good small games pick one or two and commit, which is [[scope|scope]] again, wearing a different hat.
- **It connects to [[mda|MDA]].** Different types want different aesthetics: Achievers want challenge and completion, Explorers want discovery, Socializers want fellowship, Killers want competition. MDA's own catalog of aesthetics (LeBlanc's "eight kinds of fun") is a richer descendant of exactly this "different players, different fun" insight. Bartle is the pocket version you can hold in your head.
- **It connects to [[player-promises|player promises]].** You make different promises to different types, and a lot of "this game just isn't for me" is really "this game promised the Achiever's experience and I am an Explorer." Neither player is wrong. Knowing which one you are promising to is half of knowing what to build.
