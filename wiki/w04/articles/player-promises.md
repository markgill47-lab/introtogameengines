# Player Promises

Start a game you have never played, and within about a minute you know, without anyone telling you, roughly what the next ten hours are going to be. A health bar means you are going to fight. A dialogue wheel means your choices are supposed to matter. A vast map with a fog of war means exploring is the point. You did not read a manual. The game *promised*, and you heard it.

Every game makes promises the moment it starts, and most of them are unspoken. The [[genre-tour|genre]] makes them, the [[mda|mechanics]] you are taught make them, the art and the music make them, and above all the first hour makes them: the first hour is a promissory note for the shape of the rest. This is the honest version of the [[mda|designer-and-player]] relationship. The designer works in mechanics, the player lives in aesthetics, and the promise is the thread strung across that gap, carrying "here is what I am going to make you feel" from one end to the other.

## Chekhov's gun, in a game

The playwright's rule: a rifle hung on the wall in the first act must be fired by the third, or it should not be on the wall. Games are full of guns on walls. A locked door promises a key. A big empty arena promises a boss. An item you cannot afford yet promises a reason to save. A skill tree promises that the choices in it are real. Hang the gun, fire the gun. When a game shows you something and never pays it off, a small part of the player keeps waiting, and "waiting for a thing that never comes" is not the aesthetic you were going for.

## Keeping promises is the trust

When a game keeps its promises, the player relaxes and trusts it, and trust is the real currency of the whole experience. Break a promise and the player feels betrayed, even when they cannot name what happened. That "this feels cheap," "this feels unfair" reaction is almost always a broken promise, and it is a clean [[mda|MDA trace]]: the aesthetic (feels unfair) traces to a dynamic (I did exactly what the game taught me and it punished me anyway) traces to a mechanic (a rule that quietly contradicted an earlier rule). A stealth game that teaches you patience and then forces a loud mandatory boss broke a promise. A difficulty spike with no warning broke a promise. An unwinnable fight the game never signaled broke a promise. Every one of them reads as unfairness, and every one is really a contract violation.

## The designer's actual job

Most of design, from the player's side, is promise management, and it has three parts:

1. **Know what you are promising**, because you are always promising more than you meant to. Every asset, every tutorial, every genre signal is a clause in a contract you may not have realized you signed.
2. **Keep the promises you make.** Consistency, fairness, follow-through. The gun fires.
3. **Do not make promises your [[scope|scope]] cannot cash.** A design doc that promises an open world, crafting, and multiplayer has written a note it cannot pay, and the player who believed the box art is the one left holding it. The small promise, overdelivered, beats the big promise, defaulted on. Every time.

## Which is exactly what malice inverts

This is why [[malicious-design|malicious design]] feels like a betrayal and not just a bill. The free hook *promises* generosity; the wall breaks it. The onboarding *promises* "this is the game"; the difficulty spike reveals a different, meaner game that was always the plan. The broken promise is not a side effect of the monetization. It **is** the monetization. So when a game turns on you, do not just feel wronged: name the promise it broke. The honest designer makes a small promise and overdelivers. The malicious one makes a big promise and defaults, on purpose, for ten dollars.
