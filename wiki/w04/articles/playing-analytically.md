# How to Play Analytically

Playing a game as a designer is a different activity from playing it as a player, and the difference is mostly *when you write things down*.

1. **First pass: just play.** Twenty minutes, no notes. You need the player experience before you dissect it, because [[mda|the aesthetics layer]] only exists while you are inside it.
2. **Second pass: play with a notepad.** Now you are on the clock:
   - **Time the core loop.** Literally count seconds between repetitions of the central action. Write the loop as one sentence.
   - **List the verbs.** Everything the game lets you *do*. Be strict: "explore" is usually three real verbs wearing a trenchcoat.
   - **Follow the resources.** What do you gain, spend, and convert? Include the sneaky ones: time, space, information.
   - **Catch a feedback loop in the act.** Find one moment where doing well made things easier (positive) or doing badly triggered help (negative). Games contain both more often than you would think.
   - **Log your own feelings with timestamps.** Bored at minute 12, tense at minute 20. Your feelings are aesthetics data, and the timestamps let you trace them back through dynamics to a mechanic. That trace is the whole skill.
3. **Third pass (optional, recommended): break it.** Play *wrong* on purpose. Hoard when you should spend, run past enemies, refuse the tutorial weapon. Where the game holds up anyway, the systems are deep. Where it falls apart, you have found the assumptions the designer made about you, and finding those is expert-level deconstruction.
