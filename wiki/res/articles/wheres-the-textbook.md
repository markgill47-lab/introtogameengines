# Where's the Textbook?

The question worth asking out loud: is there one open-source textbook on game engines that covers this course's full arc (engine anatomy, scene graphs, the loop, physics, UI and state, cameras and feel, animation, patterns, data-driven design, procgen, game AI, design vocabulary)?

**The short answer: no.** The canonical comprehensive book (Gregory's *Game Engine Architecture*) is commercial, and nothing in the open-education world matches its scope. It's also a 1,200-page AAA-engine-internals book aimed well above a first-course-after-Programming-2 audience. Good instructor bookshelf material; wrong assigned text for SE 266.

But the course's topics map remarkably well onto a small stack of genuinely free texts, one of which is practically the patterns tier in book form. So the model this course uses is a **companion-texts stack**: named free texts, linked per week, with the course's own wiki modules as the connective tissue.

The one-line version: *there is no textbook. There are five excellent free ones, each assigned where it's strongest, and the course modules are the map between them.*

## What no open text covers

Worth knowing where the stack runs out, because those gaps are what the course's own modules exist for:

- **Game feel and juice** (Week 7): the canonical materials are GDC talks, free on YouTube, not texts. See [[game-feel|Game Feel, On Video]].
- **UI and game state discipline** (Week 6), **data-driven design and persistence as a teaching unit** (Week 13), and the **Standard Prefab** house style ([[w02:standard-prefab|introduced in Week 2]]): scattered across blog posts at best. The weekly wiki modules are the text for these.

*The stack itself starts at [[nystrom|Game Programming Patterns]]. The week-by-week pairing is in [[reading-by-week|Reading by Week]].*
